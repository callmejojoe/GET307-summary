AI Ethics & Governance: The Machine Has No Conscience

Compiled from GET 307 Teaching Dossier · SIPET, FUTMINNA · 2025/26 Session
Section 1

What Is AI Ethics?

AI ethics is a multidisciplinary field that studies how to optimize AI's beneficial impact while reducing risks and adverse outcomes. It encompasses philosophy, law, sociology, and engineering — and it is not optional. As AI systems increasingly make consequential decisions about people's lives (loans, healthcare, hiring, criminal justice), the ethical dimensions of these systems matter as much as their technical performance.

AI ethics principles are applied through a system of AI governance — oversight mechanisms and guardrails that help ensure AI tools and systems remain safe and ethical in practice. Effective AI governance requires the involvement of a wide range of stakeholders: developers, users, policymakers, and ethicists.

🗞️ Analogy: The All-Knowing Judge Who Never Went to Law School

Imagine an AI system used to determine bail amounts in criminal cases. It's fast, consistent, and has no bad days. But if it was trained on 20 years of historical decisions made by judges who were themselves biased against certain ethnic groups, the AI will encode and amplify those biases at machine speed and scale — with the added problem that no one can easily explain why it made a particular decision. Speed and efficiency are meaningless if the underlying judgments are unjust.

Section 2

AI Challenges and Risks: A Classification

Organizations deploying AI face risks across four major categories:

⚠ 1. Data Risks

AI systems rely on data that may be vulnerable to:
Data poisoning: Attackers deliberately corrupting training data to cause misclassification.
Data tampering: Modification of data inputs to manipulate outputs.
Data bias: Training data that doesn't representatively sample all relevant groups.
Data breaches: Unauthorized access to sensitive training datasets.

⚠ 2. Model Risks

Model theft: Reverse-engineering a model's architecture, weights, or behavior through adversarial queries.
Model tampering: Attackers manipulate model parameters post-deployment to alter behavior.
Adversarial attacks: Carefully crafted inputs (imperceptible to humans) that fool the model into wrong predictions.

⚠ 3. Operational Risks

Model drift: Real-world data patterns shift over time; a model trained on 2020 data may perform poorly in 2025 without retraining.
Algorithmic bias: Systematic unfairness that emerges from biased training data or flawed model design.
Governance failures: Unclear accountability for model decisions and outcomes.

⚠ 4. Ethics & Legal Risks

Privacy violations: Processing personal data without proper consent or in violation of data protection laws.
Discriminatory outcomes: AI systems that systematically disadvantage protected groups.
Regulatory non-compliance: Failure to meet standards set by frameworks like GDPR, CCPA, and emerging AI-specific regulations.

Section 3

The Six Pillars of Responsible AI

The GET 307 course identifies six core values that form the foundation of ethical and responsible AI development and deployment:

📖 Pillar 1: Explainability & Interpretability

As AI becomes more advanced, humans struggle to understand how algorithms reach their conclusions — the "black box" problem. Explainable AI (XAI) is a set of processes and methods that enable human users to interpret, comprehend, and trust the results created by algorithms.

Engineering relevance: When an AI system recommends shutting down a critical machine, an engineer must be able to understand why — to verify the recommendation, override it when appropriate, and maintain accountability.

📖 Pillar 2: Fairness & Inclusion

Machine learning is inherently a form of statistical discrimination — it creates groups and treats them differently based on learned patterns. Discrimination becomes ethically objectionable when it systematically advantages privileged groups and disadvantages protected groups, causing real harm.

Practitioners can promote fairness by: auditing training data for bias, testing model outputs across demographic groups, building diverse development teams, and using algorithmic fairness constraints during training.

📖 Pillar 3: Robustness & Security

A robust AI system effectively handles exceptional conditions — abnormal inputs, edge cases, adversarial attacks, and distribution shifts — without causing unintentional harm or making dangerously wrong predictions.

Robustness testing includes: adversarial example testing, stress testing with out-of-distribution inputs, monitoring for model drift, and implementing fallback mechanisms when the model's confidence is low.

📖 Pillar 4: Accountability & Transparency

Organizations must implement clear responsibilities and governance structures for AI development, deployment, and outcomes. Who is accountable when an AI system causes harm? The developer? The organization that deployed it? The user who relied on its output?

Transparency means users can see how an AI service works, evaluate its functionality, and understand its strengths and limitations — including when it is likely to be wrong.

📖 Pillar 5: Privacy & Regulatory Compliance

Many regulatory frameworks mandate that organizations follow strict privacy principles when processing personal information used in AI:

  • GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation, EU): Right to explanation of automated decisions, right to be forgotten, data minimization
  • CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act): Right to know what data is collected; right to delete
  • Nigeria's NDPR (Nigeria Data Protection Regulation): Governs data processing of Nigerian citizens
📖 Pillar 6: Human-Centred Design & Societal Benefit

The ultimate goal of AI systems should be to benefit humanity — not merely to optimize a metric. This means considering the full societal impacts of AI deployment: job displacement, environmental costs of large-scale model training, concentration of AI power in few organizations, and the accessibility of AI benefits across different socioeconomic groups.

Section 4

Algorithmic Bias: Types, Causes, and Mitigations

Bias TypeWhere It OccursExampleMitigation
Historical Bias Training data reflects past human prejudices Hiring AI trained on data where 90% of engineers were male predicts males more suitable Audit data representation; use debiasing techniques; diverse training data
Sampling Bias Training data not representative of deployment population Medical AI trained only on data from one ethnicity performs poorly on others Stratified sampling; collect diverse, representative datasets
Measurement Bias Features used as proxies for sensitive attributes Using zip code (correlates with race) as a feature in loan decisions Audit feature correlations with protected attributes; use fairness-aware feature selection
Feedback Loop Bias Model predictions influence future training data Predictive policing model predicts high crime in area → more policing there → more arrests → more data supporting the prediction Monitor downstream effects of AI decisions; break feedback loops with human review
🗞️ Analogy: The Map that Makes the Territory

Normally, the map represents the territory. But feedback loop bias is like a map that changes the territory to match itself. If a crime prediction map marks a neighborhood as high-risk, police concentrate there, find more crime (because they're looking), and this validates the map's prediction — creating a self-fulfilling prophecy. The map isn't accurate; it's actively shaping what it claims to describe.

Section 5

Implementing AI Governance: A Practitioner's Framework

  1. Pre-Deployment: Ethics Impact Assessment

    Before training any model, conduct a structured assessment of potential harms: Who could be adversely affected? What data is being used? What regulatory requirements apply? Document risks and mitigation strategies.

  2. During Development: Bias Auditing & Fairness Testing

    Regularly audit training data for bias. Test model performance across all demographic subgroups — not just overall accuracy. Apply algorithmic fairness constraints if needed.

  3. Deployment: Transparency & Human Oversight

    Clearly communicate to users when AI is making decisions affecting them. Maintain human override mechanisms for high-stakes decisions. Document model limitations prominently.

  4. Post-Deployment: Monitoring & Accountability

    Continuously monitor for model drift, bias emergence, and unexpected behaviors. Establish clear accountability chains: who is responsible when the model causes harm? Conduct regular audits.

  5. Incident Response: When Things Go Wrong

    Have a documented incident response plan for when AI systems cause harm. This includes: model rollback procedures, stakeholder notification, root cause analysis, and corrective action documentation.

"Organizations should implement clear responsibilities and governance structures for the development, deployment, and outcomes of AI systems."

Section 6

AI Ethics in Engineering Practice

As engineering students, you will build systems that affect real people. The GET 307 course explicitly requires you to integrate responsible AI principles — data privacy, fairness, explainability, and security — into your analyses and project work.

Consider a mechatronics engineer deploying a predictive maintenance AI on a factory floor. The ethical questions are not abstract:

Real Engineering Ethics Questions

Worker safety: If the AI incorrectly predicts "no maintenance needed" and a machine fails catastrophically, who is liable? Was the model adequately tested?

Job displacement: If the AI automates the work of experienced maintenance technicians, how was this disclosed to workers? Were they retrained?

Data privacy: If the system uses worker behavior data (logged keystrokes, movement patterns) as features, did workers consent to this data collection?

Transparency: When the AI recommends an emergency shutdown, can it explain why in terms a maintenance engineer can verify and act on?

📰 Quiz — Topic VI: AI Ethics & Governance

5 Questions · Select & Submit

Question 01

An AI hiring tool is trained on historical hiring data from a company where 90% of hires were male. It subsequently rates female candidates lower for technical roles. This is primarily an example of:

Correct: C. Historical bias occurs when training data reflects past human discrimination. The model learns that "male + technical role = hire" is the pattern, because that's what happened historically — not because it's the correct or fair pattern. This is one of the most common and consequential AI ethics failures.

Question 02

What is "Explainable AI" (XAI) and why is it important in engineering applications?

Correct: B. XAI is about making AI decisions understandable and verifiable to humans. In engineering, this is critical: an engineer must be able to understand WHY a predictive maintenance system flagged a component for replacement, so they can validate, challenge, or override the recommendation with domain expertise.

Question 03

A predictive policing AI flags a neighborhood as high-crime. More police are deployed there, leading to more arrests, which adds to the training data supporting the prediction. This scenario illustrates which type of bias?

Correct: D. Feedback loop bias (also called automation bias or self-fulfilling prophecy bias) occurs when a model's predictions influence the data that will be used to re-train it, creating a self-reinforcing cycle. The model doesn't get corrected — it gets confirmed, regardless of whether its predictions were accurate.

Question 04

GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) places which of the following obligations on organizations using AI with personal data?

Correct: A. GDPR's key provisions relevant to AI include: (1) Data minimization — only collect what's necessary; (2) Right to explanation — subjects can request explanations of automated decisions affecting them; (3) Right to erasure — individuals can request deletion of their personal data.

Question 05

Which of the following best describes "model drift" and why it poses an operational risk in deployed AI systems?

Correct: C. Model drift (also called concept drift) occurs because the world changes — consumer behaviors shift, diseases mutate, economic conditions evolve — but a deployed model's parameters stay frozen at their training state. Without monitoring and retraining, model performance silently degrades, sometimes catastrophically. Option D describes model tampering (a security threat).
