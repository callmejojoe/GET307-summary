Machine Learning: Teaching Machines to Learn

Compiled from GET 307 lecture notes · Dr. I.M. Simeon, ABE Dept., FUTMINNA
Section 1

What Is Machine Learning?

Machine learning (ML) sits directly underneath AI in the technology hierarchy. It involves creating models by training an algorithm to make predictions or decisions based on data. The revolutionary idea: instead of programmers writing every rule, the machine discovers the rules itself from examples.

ML encompasses a broad range of techniques that enable computers to learn from and make inferences based on data without being explicitly programmed for specific tasks. Popular ML algorithms include linear regression, logistic regression, decision trees, random forest, support vector machines (SVMs), k-nearest neighbor (KNN), and clustering.

🗞️ Analogy: Learning to Recognize Mangoes

Traditional programming: you write rules — "if oval-shaped AND yellow-orange AND heavy for its size, THEN it's a mango." But what if the mango is green? Or small? Your rules break. Machine learning instead shows the algorithm 10,000 photos of mangoes and non-mangoes, and it learns to extract the patterns — colour gradients, shape distributions, texture — on its own. New fruit variety? It adapts.

Section 2

The Four Paradigms of Machine Learning

ML algorithms are typically categorised by the type of feedback the learning system receives during training. There are four major paradigms:

Paradigm 1: Supervised Learning

The simplest and most common form of ML. The algorithm trains on a labelled dataset — each training example is paired with an output label by a human. The goal is for the model to learn the mapping between inputs and outputs, so it can predict the labels of new, unseen data.

Examples: Email spam detection (spam/not spam), house price prediction, disease diagnosis (positive/negative).

🗞️ Analogy: The Student with an Answer Key

Supervised learning is like a student studying with a practice exam that includes an answer key. For every question, they can check whether they got it right, and they adjust their understanding until their answers consistently match the key. The "labelled data" is the answer key. At exam time (test data), no key — they're on their own.

Paradigm 2: Unsupervised Learning

The algorithm is given data without any labels and must discover structure, patterns, or groupings on its own. The model figures out what categories or clusters exist in the data without being told what to look for.

Examples: Customer segmentation, topic modelling in documents, anomaly detection, dimensionality reduction.

🗞️ Analogy: Sorting Laundry Without Instructions

Unsupervised learning is like sorting a huge pile of laundry without anyone telling you the categories. You naturally group by colour, fabric type, or size — discovering the structure yourself. No one labelled the clothes; you found the patterns. A clustering algorithm does exactly this with data points.

Paradigm 3: Semi-Supervised Learning

Combines supervised and unsupervised learning by using both labelled and unlabelled data to train models. This is particularly useful when labelling data is expensive or time-consuming — you label a small portion and let the model use unlabelled examples to improve further.

Example: Medical image classification where only some scans have expert diagnoses.

Paradigm 4: Reinforcement Learning (RL)

An agent learns by trial-and-error and a reward function rather than from labelled data. The agent takes actions in an environment and receives positive or negative feedback (rewards/penalties), gradually learning to take actions that maximise cumulative reward.

Examples: Game-playing AI (AlphaGo, chess engines), robot locomotion, autonomous driving, recommendation systems.

🗞️ Analogy: Training a Puppy

Reinforcement learning works exactly like training a dog. You don't write a manual explaining "how to sit." You reward the dog with a treat when it sits and withhold the treat (or say "no") when it doesn't. Over many trials, the dog learns to sit because sitting reliably produces treats. The AI agent is the dog; the reward signal is the treat.

Also: Self-Supervised & Transfer Learning

Self-supervised learning generates implicit labels from unstructured data, rather than relying on human-labelled datasets. Large language models like GPT are trained this way — the next word in a sentence is the "label."

Transfer learning reuses knowledge gained from training on one task to improve performance on a different but related task. Instead of training from scratch, you start with a pre-trained model. This is why GPT can be fine-tuned for medical diagnosis without retraining from zero.

Section 3

Neural Networks: The Brain-Inspired Architecture

One of the most popular ML model types is the neural network (or artificial neural network, ANN). Neural networks are modelled after the human brain's structure — specifically the web of billions of neurons connected by synapses.

An ANN consists of interconnected layers of nodes (analogous to neurons) that work together to process complex data. Each connection has a weight: a number representing how strongly one node influences another. Training adjusts these weights until the network produces correct outputs.

Section 4

Deep Learning: Going Deeper

Deep learning is a subset of machine learning that uses multilayer neural networks (called deep neural networks, DNNs) — networks with at least three hidden layers, and often hundreds of them.

While a classic ML neural network might have one or two hidden layers, deep neural networks can have hundreds. This depth is what makes them powerful: each layer learns progressively more abstract features. In image recognition:

  • Layer 1 → Detects edges and colour gradients
  • Layer 2 → Detects shapes made of edges
  • Layer 3 → Detects object parts (eyes, wheels, leaves)
  • Final Layers → Identifies the whole object (a face, a car, a plant)

This multiple-layer structure enables unsupervised feature extraction: deep networks can automatically extract meaningful patterns from raw, unlabelled data without humans having to tell them what features to look for.

Because deep learning doesn't require manual feature engineering or constant human intervention, it scales enormously. Some form of deep learning powers most of the AI applications in our lives today.

🗞️ Analogy: The Junior vs Senior Detective

A classic ML model with one hidden layer is like a junior detective given a checklist: "Check for blood type. Check for fingerprints. Check for motive." They follow the list a human gave them. A deep learning model with hundreds of layers is like a senior detective who has solved 10,000 cases. They intuitively notice things the checklist never mentioned — the nervous twitch, the slightly-too-clean crime scene — because their deep experience taught them what to look for. No one gave them that checklist; they built it themselves.

Section 5

Generative AI: Machines That Create

Generative AI (gen AI) refers to deep learning models that can create complex original content — long-form text, high-quality images, realistic video and audio — in response to a user's prompt.

At a high level, generative models encode a simplified representation of their training data, then draw from that representation to create new work that's similar — but not identical — to the original. Think of them as learning the statistical "grammar" of existing content, then using it to write new sentences.

Three major deep learning architectures underpin modern generative AI:

Architecture 1: Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) — Introduced 2013

VAEs learn a compressed "latent space" representation of training data and can generate multiple variations of content in response to a prompt or instruction.

Architecture 2: Diffusion Models — First Seen 2014

Diffusion models add "noise" to images progressively until they're unrecognisable, then learn to reverse this process — generating original images by "denoising" from random noise. This powers image generators like DALL-E and Stable Diffusion.

Architecture 3: Transformers — The Dominant Architecture

Trained on sequenced data to generate extended sequences of content (words in sentences, frames in video, commands in code). Transformers are at the core of today's most powerful generative AI tools: ChatGPT / GPT-4, Google Gemini, Anthropic Claude, BERT, Midjourney, GitHub Copilot.

Section 6

How Generative AI Works: Three Phases

  1. Phase 1: Training (Creating the Foundation Model)

    A deep learning algorithm is trained on enormous volumes of raw, unstructured, unlabelled data — terabytes to petabytes from the internet. This produces a foundation model: a neural network of billions of parameters encoding representations of entities, patterns, and relationships. This phase is compute-intensive, time-consuming, and expensive — often requiring thousands of GPUs and costing millions of USD.

  2. Phase 2: Tuning (Adapting to a Specific Task)

    The foundation model is refined for a specific application using: Fine-tuning (feeding the model labelled, domain-specific data) or RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (humans evaluate model outputs and the model adjusts to improve based on their feedback — how ChatGPT learned to be helpful and safe).

  3. Phase 3: Generation, Evaluation & More Tuning

    Developers and users regularly assess outputs and retune the model — as often as weekly. The foundation model itself is updated less frequently (every year or 18 months). Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) can also extend the model with external knowledge sources without full retraining.

Section 7

AI Agents: Beyond Chatbots

An AI agent is an autonomous AI program that can perform tasks and accomplish goals without human intervention, by designing its own workflow and using available tools (other applications or services).

Agentic AI is a system of multiple AI agents whose efforts are coordinated to accomplish a more complex task than any single agent could achieve alone.

🗞️ Analogy: The Chatbot vs The Personal Assistant

A chatbot (gen AI) can tell you: "The best time to climb Mount Everest is in May, before the monsoon season." An AI agent can do the same — and then autonomously book your flight, reserve your hotel in Kathmandu, schedule your altitude acclimatization hikes, and add everything to your calendar. Same intelligence; vastly more capability because it can take actions in the world, not just generate text.

ML Paradigms Summary

  • Supervised: Labelled data → predict labels
  • Unsupervised: No labels → find patterns
  • Semi-supervised: Both labelled & unlabelled
  • Reinforcement: Trial, error, and rewards
  • Self-supervised: Labels from data itself
  • Transfer: Reuse knowledge across tasks

Key Terms

  • ANN: Artificial Neural Network
  • DNN: Deep Neural Network (3+ hidden layers)
  • LLM: Large Language Model (GPT, Gemini)
  • RAG: Retrieval Augmented Generation
  • RLHF: RL from Human Feedback
  • Foundation Model: Massive pre-trained base model
  • Fine-tuning: Adapting a base model for a task

Gen AI Architectures

  • VAEs (2013) — variations from latent space
  • Diffusion Models (2014) — denoise to generate
  • Transformers — sequences; powers ChatGPT
  • GPT-4, Gemini, Claude = Transformer LLMs
  • Midjourney = Diffusion + Transformer

5 Questions · Select & Submit

Question 01

What is the defining characteristic of supervised machine learning?

Correct: C. Supervised learning requires labelled data — humans pair each training example with an output label. The model learns the input-to-output mapping so it can predict labels for unseen data. Option D describes Reinforcement Learning; B describes Unsupervised Learning.

Question 02

How does a deep neural network (DNN) differ from a standard neural network in classical machine learning?

Correct: D. The "deep" in deep learning refers to the number of hidden layers. Classical NNs have 1–2 hidden layers. DNNs have at least 3, often hundreds. This depth enables automatic feature extraction from raw, unlabelled data at scale.

Question 03

Which generative AI architecture powers most of today's headline-making large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini?

Correct: A. Transformers are the dominant architecture for LLMs. They process entire sequences in parallel (unlike RNNs) and their attention mechanism lets them relate distant words in context. VAEs and Diffusion models are primarily used for image generation.

Question 04

Reinforcement learning (RL) is best described by which of the following?

Correct: B. RL involves an agent, an environment, actions, and a reward function. The agent learns by exploring actions and receiving feedback — positive (reward) for good actions, negative (penalty) for bad ones. AlphaGo was trained with RL. Option D describes Transfer Learning.

Question 05

What is Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) used for in the context of generative AI?

Correct: C. RAG improves a gen AI app's accuracy by allowing it to retrieve relevant information from external sources (documents, databases) at inference time, supplementing what the model learned during training. This is cheaper than retraining and keeps knowledge current.
