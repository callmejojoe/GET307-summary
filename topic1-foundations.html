Foundations of Artificial Intelligence

Compiled from GET 307 lecture notes · Dr. I.M. Simeon, ABE Dept., FUTMINNA
Section 1

What Is Artificial Intelligence?

Artificial intelligence is technology that enables computers and machines to simulate human learning, comprehension, problem-solving, decision-making, creativity, and autonomy. In simpler terms: it is software that can think — at least in the functional sense — so that it can act intelligently without being given explicit step-by-step instructions for every possible situation.

AI-powered applications and devices can see and identify objects (computer vision), understand and respond to human language (natural language processing), learn from new information and experience (machine learning), make detailed recommendations, and act independently — the classic example being a self-driving car.

🗞️ Analogy: The Apprentice Chef

Think of traditional programming like giving a chef a rigid recipe book. Every dish must be cooked exactly as written. If an ingredient is missing, the chef is stuck. Artificial intelligence is like training an apprentice: you show them hundreds of dishes, explain flavour principles, and over time they develop the judgment to improvise when an ingredient is missing — and even invent new recipes. The apprentice learned from examples, not a fixed rulebook.

Section 2

The Three-Layer Hierarchy: AI → ML → Deep Learning

These three terms are often used interchangeably, but they are nested concepts — think of them as concentric circles, where each inner circle is a more specialized subset of the one outside it.

Level What It Is Analogy
Artificial Intelligence The broad field of making machines behave intelligently The whole ocean
Machine Learning AI systems that learn patterns from data A region of the ocean
Deep Learning ML using multi-layered neural networks A deep trench within that region
Section 3

Weak AI vs. Strong AI

To contextualize AI complexity, researchers distinguish between two types based on their scope and sophistication:

📖 Definition: Weak AI (Narrow AI)

AI systems designed to perform a specific task or set of tasks. Examples include Amazon's Alexa (voice commands), Apple's Siri, social media recommendation engines, and Tesla's autopilot. These systems can be superhuman within their narrow domain but are completely helpless outside it. Alexa cannot drive a car; Tesla's autopilot cannot answer a customer service query.

📖 Definition: Strong AI (Artificial General Intelligence — AGI)

A hypothetical AI with the ability to understand, learn, and apply knowledge across any task at a level equal to or surpassing human intelligence. No known AI system currently approaches this level of sophistication. Researchers debate whether AGI is even possible without fundamentally new architectures. The self-aware robots of science fiction remain firmly in that realm.

🗞️ Analogy: The Specialist vs The Generalist Doctor

Weak AI is like a brilliant cardiologist: extraordinary at diagnosing heart conditions, but if you come in with a broken leg, they're lost. Strong AI would be the mythical doctor who is equally expert in cardiology, neurology, orthopaedics, and surgery — and can also cook your dinner and compose a symphony. That doctor doesn't exist yet.

Section 4

A Brief History of AI

The concept of "a machine that thinks" is ancient, but the modern scientific field of AI has key milestones worth knowing for your exams:

  • 1950
    The Turing Test

    Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," posing the famous question: "Can machines think?" He proposes the Turing Test to evaluate machine intelligence through conversation.

  • 1956
    AI Is Born at Dartmouth

    John McCarthy coins the term "artificial intelligence" at the Dartmouth Conference. Allen Newell, J.C. Shaw and Herbert Simon create the Logic Theorist, the first running AI program.

  • 1967
    The First Neural Network

    Frank Rosenblatt builds the Mark 1 Perceptron — a computer based on a neural network that "learned" through trial and error. A landmark, though Minsky and Papert's critique slowed further research.

  • 1980
    Backpropagation Goes Mainstream

    Neural networks using the backpropagation algorithm to train themselves become widely used in AI applications.

  • 1997
    Deep Blue Defeats Kasparov

    IBM's Deep Blue beats world chess champion Garry Kasparov. A landmark demonstration that specialized AI can surpass human experts in defined domains.

  • 2011
    Watson Wins Jeopardy!

    IBM Watson beats champions Ken Jennings and Brad Rutter at Jeopardy! Data science emerges as a discipline around this period.

  • 2016
    AlphaGo Defeats the World Champion

    DeepMind's AlphaGo beats Lee Sedol (world champion Go player) 4–1. The game has more possible moves than atoms in the universe — a stunning achievement for deep learning.

  • 2022
    The ChatGPT Moment

    OpenAI releases ChatGPT. Large Language Models (LLMs) enter mainstream use, triggering an enormous wave of interest, investment, and transformation across industries.

  • 2024
    Multimodal AI & Smaller Models

    Multimodal models that process text, images, and audio simultaneously provide richer AI experiences. Efficient smaller models challenge the "bigger is always better" assumption.

Section 5

Benefits of Artificial Intelligence

AI offers documented advantages across virtually every industry. The most significant include:

01 · Automation of Repetitive Tasks

AI can handle digital tasks (data collection, preprocessing, entry) and physical tasks (warehouse stock-picking, manufacturing line operations), freeing humans for higher-value creative work.

02 · Enhanced Decision-Making

Whether providing decision support or fully automated decisions, AI enables faster, more accurate, data-driven predictions. Combined with automation, businesses can respond to crises in real time.

03 · Reduction of Human Error

AI guides people through proper processes, flags potential errors, and in some cases fully automates error-prone tasks — critical in healthcare, engineering, and manufacturing.

04 · 24×7 Availability & Consistency

Unlike human workers, AI chatbots and automated systems never sleep, take breaks, or have bad days. They deliver consistent performance every time.

05 · Reduced Physical Risk

AI can perform dangerous work — handling explosives, deep-ocean operations, high-altitude tasks, and space exploration — eliminating the need to put human workers at risk.

Section 7

AI Challenges and Risks

The rapid adoption of AI is necessary for competitiveness, but it comes with significant challenges that every AI practitioner must understand and mitigate:

⚠ Data Risks

AI systems rely on datasets that may be vulnerable to data poisoning (attackers corrupting training data), data bias (training data that doesn't represent all groups), and data breaches. A biased training dataset produces a biased model.

⚠ Model Risks

Threat actors can target AI models for theft, reverse engineering, or manipulation. Attackers may tamper with model weights and parameters — the core components that determine its behavior and accuracy.

⚠ Operational Risks

Model drift occurs when a model's performance degrades over time as real-world data patterns change. Without monitoring and re-training, deployed models become less accurate and potentially harmful.

⚠ Ethics & Legal Risks

Biased AI hiring tools may reinforce gender or racial stereotypes. Privacy violations occur when AI processes personal data without proper consent. Regulatory frameworks like GDPR impose strict legal obligations on organizations using AI with personal data.

🗞️ Analogy: The Biased Exam Marker

Imagine a student grading system trained exclusively on essays from students at one wealthy school. When deployed across all schools, it consistently penalizes writing styles common at underfunded schools — not because the essays are worse, but because they differ from what the system learned to value. The model reflects the bias baked into its training data. This is exactly the risk with AI hiring systems trained on historical hiring data from non-diverse workforces.

Correct: B. This is the exact definition from the course material. AI enables machines to simulate human cognitive functions — it is not limited to robots, automation alone, or any programming paradigm.

Question 02

John McCarthy coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" at which event, and in which year?

Correct: C. The Dartmouth Conference in 1956 was the founding moment of AI as an academic discipline. McCarthy also went on to invent the Lisp programming language.

Question 03

Weak AI (Narrow AI) is best characterized by which of the following?

Correct: A. Weak/Narrow AI excels at specific tasks (chess, image recognition, translation) but cannot generalize outside its domain. ALL current AI systems, including ChatGPT, are Narrow AI.

Question 04

Which of the following correctly describes the relationship between AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning?

Correct: D. Think concentric circles: AI is the largest concept → ML is a subset of AI (learning from data) → Deep Learning is a subset of ML (using multi-layer neural networks).

Question 05

An AI hiring tool is trained on historical data from a company with a predominantly male workforce. When deployed, it consistently ranks female candidates lower. This scenario is an example of which AI risk?

Correct: B. Data bias occurs when the training dataset doesn't fairly represent all relevant groups. The model learns and amplifies historical human biases. This is a well-documented AI ethics problem distinct from data poisoning (which requires malicious external intent).
